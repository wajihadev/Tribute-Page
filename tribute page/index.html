About Allama Iqbal

Sir Muhammad Iqbal (1877–1938), widely known as Allama Iqbal, was a philosopher, poet, and politician in British India. A visionary who inspired the Pakistan Movement, Iqbal is celebrated as the "Poet of the East" for his profound contributions to Urdu and Persian literature. His poetry explores themes of self-realization, unity, and spiritual awakening.

Iqbal’s famous works include *Bang-e-Dra*, *Bāl-e-Jibrīl*, and *Zarb-e-Kalim*, which continue to inspire millions across the globe. His philosophy emphasized action, purpose, and a deep connection with one's faith.

Key Milestones in Iqbal's Life

  • 1877: Born in Sialkot, British India.
  • 1905-1908: Studied in Europe, earning a PhD in Philosophy.
  • 1930: Delivered the Allahabad Address, envisioning a separate Muslim state.
  • 1938: Passed away in Lahore, leaving a lasting legacy.

Iqbal's Legacy

Allama Iqbal’s contributions extend beyond literature and poetry. His vision laid the groundwork for the establishment of Pakistan, and his ideas continue to shape intellectual thought in the Muslim world.

In Pakistan, Iqbal Day is celebrated annually on November 9th to honor his life and legacy. His mausoleum in Lahore remains a symbol of national pride and reverence.
